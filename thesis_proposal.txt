Evaluating creative and technical design tasks is traditionally a subjective, time consuming, and resource intensive process that relies on panels of human experts. This thesis aims to investigate whether Agentic Artificial Intelligence can reliably replicate or replace human evaluation in such domains. The study proposes a framework in which an AI system dynamically generates multiple expert personas tailored to a specific design task. These AI “judges” will independently evaluate design submissions using the Consensual Assessment Technique (CAT), a widely used approach for assessing creative work based on expert consensus.

The research will use a publicly available dataset of human generated design tasks, such as product sketches or concept ideas, that already includes evaluation data from human expert panels. Each AI persona will evaluate the dataset across six dimensions on a 0 to 5 scale: Creativity, Originality, Usefulness or Relevance, Clarity, Level of Detail, and Feasibility. To assess reliability, statistical methods such as the Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) will be applied to calculate Inter Rater Reliability (IRR), alongside Variance Analysis to measure the score spread and consensus deviation among the AI judges. This will allow comparison both among the AI personas and between the AI panel and the human baseline evaluations.

The system will be implemented using a modern web application architecture, featuring a React-based frontend and a Python FastAPI backend for API integration and data processing. The core evaluation engine relies on a concurrent, multi-agent AI pipeline structured as a "3x3 Fan-Out" matrix. A "Recruiter Agent" will first analyze the design context to dynamically generate three distinct, domain-specific evaluator personas. These personas will then be instantiated simultaneously across commercial Large Language Model APIs, such as OpenAI, xAI (Grok), and Anthropic, triggering nine parallel, asynchronous evaluations. Finally, a "Synthesizer Agent" will aggregate the independent judgments and compile a consensus report. For statistical analysis, Python libraries including Pandas, NumPy, and Pingouin will be used to process the data, compute the required reliability metrics (such as two-way mixed ICC), and perform Variance Analysis to detect score divergence and identify which specific personas or dimensions cause the most disagreement.

The expected outcome of this research is a clear and statistically supported comparison between Agentic AI and human evaluators in subjective design assessment. The results are expected to identify which evaluation dimensions are closely aligned with human judgment and where differences occur. Overall, the study aims to determine whether a multi persona Agentic AI system can serve as a reliable and scalable replacement, or at least a strong supporting tool, for human evaluation in academic and professional design environments.
